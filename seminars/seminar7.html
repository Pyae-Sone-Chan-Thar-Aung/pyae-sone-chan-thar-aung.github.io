Seminar

Prompt Enginnering for online and offline Large Language Models

Speaker: Mr. Eric Emberda

Date: May 28, 2025

Location: UIC Computer Lab

Reflection

The seminar on "Prompt Engineering for Online and Offline Large Language Models" was a highly informative and engaging session that expanded our understanding of how AI chatbots function, both online and offline. The speaker compared various AI-powered tools including ChatGPT, DeepSeek, Gemini, Claude, and Copilot, highlighting their strengths and differences. One of the key focuses was on how prompt engineering is used to guide these models in generating meaningful and accurate responses. Techniques like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), fine-tuning, and various prompting strategies such as chain-of-thought, zero-shot, and few-shot prompting were explored in depth. These methods help bridge the gap between raw model capability and practical, reliable output.

Among the techniques discussed, RAG stood out for its ability to ground AI responses using external knowledge sources like databases or documents, thus reducing hallucinations and increasing accuracy. Fine-tuning was another strategy that resonated with me—it’s amazing how tailoring a model to a specific dataset, like legal or medical documents, can drastically improve its usefulness in specialized domains. Prompt engineering itself feels like a form of art and science, where even subtle changes in how we phrase our inputs can significantly affect the results we get from the model.

What made the seminar especially engaging was the hands-on demonstration of offline language models using tools like Ollama, OpenWebUI, Docker Desktop, and the local endpoint access via https://localhost:11343. Seeing the setup in action through the command terminal helped us understand that powerful AI tools are not limited to cloud-based services. With the right configuration, we can deploy and run language models on our local machines, which opens up a lot of opportunities for privacy-sensitive or offline applications. This practical demonstration bridged the theoretical with the tangible and made the concept of running LLMs more accessible to us students.

Personally, I found the idea of personalizing prompts to help the AI better understand us very powerful. For example, when I asked the model to give advice on managing study time based on my routine, I realized that a well-crafted prompt can truly make the AI feel more intelligent and relevant. The speaker encouraged us to think like prompt engineers—experiment, tweak, and refine how we interact with these models. This mindset, along with the knowledge we gained, gave me a deeper appreciation for how language models work and how we can harness them responsibly, both in personal use and in future professional or research projects.

Key Takeaways

  • Prompt engineering techniques like RAG, fine-tuning, and zero/few-shot prompting enhance AI output quality.
  • RAG allows models to retrieve external data, improving response accuracy and reducing hallucinations.
  • Offline AI models using tools like Ollama and Docker can run locally, supporting privacy-sensitive use cases.
  • Effective prompt design greatly influences how language models understand and respond to input.
  • Personalized prompts and experimentation help users maximize the potential of AI chatbots.
